Every January, I become convinced that I am going to write a book.

There are actually several books. I have written hundreds of pages of some of them. I begin with the wonderful certainty that someone somewhere might want this information—that something I have learned about bodies, consciousness, behavior, or suffering might help another person. Then, a few months later, I get in my own way. I decide no one will read it. I decide I am silly to believe I have anything interesting to say. I imagine people finding me pedantic and preachy. Are those the same thing? It hardly matters. Either one is usually enough to stop me.

But the story I tell about “every January” is not quite true. One January, I was about as far from writing a book as I could be. I was sleeping on a friend’s couch. I woke in the middle of the night and cried for hours. I berated myself for ever believing I could say anything another person might take seriously.

My mental health has always been dicey. A difficult childhood led into a tumultuous early adulthood. I live with complex post-traumatic stress, anxiety, and dissociative symptoms. I have accomplished wonderful things while carrying those conditions, but I am also labile. I have mood swings. I sometimes have difficulty censoring myself. Loud noises and crowds can leave me disoriented. Over the years, I have used prescribed and unprescribed substances to manage what I did not know how to hold. Alcohol became a way to sleep. Opiates, and later kratom, offered relief of another kind. The addictions generated shame, and the shame worsened the symptoms that made relief feel necessary.

Through much of it, I remained “high functioning,” a phrase we use when suffering has not yet inconvenienced everyone else.

Then my life collapsed. On December 23, 2023, my husband called the police, and I was arrested for domestic violence. I spent three days in jail. When I got out, I could not go home. My access to our bank accounts had been cut off. I was homeless and destitute. A friend took me in and helped me begin again.

I know how this sounds. Another unstable woman with a life on fire who thinks she has found an answer to happiness. That description makes me smile now. At the time, it nearly killed me.

I do not tell this story to present suffering as a credential. Pain does not automatically make a person wise, and survival does not prove that everything happens for a reason. I tell it because knowledge is never produced from nowhere. We speak from bodies with histories. Mine has known disability, trauma, addiction, professional achievement, motherhood, fear, and the strange social demand to hide all but the most flattering parts.

For years, I believed that these contradictions disqualified me. How could I write about resilience when I had fallen apart? How could I help people trust their bodies when I had spent years trying to escape mine? How could I speak about happiness when I had been so desperately unhappy?

The question contains its own mistake. It assumes that knowledge belongs only to people who have mastered life. It imagines wisdom as a clean product delivered by a clean person from a safe distance. But no one lives at a safe distance from being human. A dietitian is still hungry. A philosopher is still frightened. A person who understands the nervous system can still be overwhelmed by her own.

What I know did not prevent my life from falling apart. It did help me live through the collapse. My understanding of the brain, the body, consciousness, learning, and philosophy gave me ways to interpret what was happening without reducing myself to it. I could see that a nervous system shaped by danger would continue to predict danger. I could understand the impulse to numb without calling it evidence of a defective soul. I could recognize that shame feels personal while being built from years of relationships, environments, and cultural judgments.

Understanding did not make the pain disappear. It made the pain more knowable. And what becomes knowable often becomes less frightening.

This distinction sits at the center of my work. We are taught to divide ourselves into a thinking self and a troublesome body. The self is expected to command; the body is expected to obey. When it does not—when it hungers, gains weight, panics, becomes ill, craves relief, or collapses—we interpret the response as betrayal. We believe the body has failed us, or that we have failed to control it.

But a body is not a subordinate object. It is the living process through which a world has happened to us and through which we can respond. Its reactions have histories. Its adaptations have causes. Even the responses that now hurt us once emerged from conditions that made them possible, necessary, or at least more likely.

This is not a promise that understanding will produce perfect health or permanent happiness. I do not have a magic answer. Happiness is not an object waiting in the right career, relationship, body size, medication, or philosophy. It is something assembled from moments of safety, connection, attention, and meaning. It is constrained by material reality. It is affected by biology. It can be made more possible, but it cannot be commanded.

I am writing because a life does not have to be tidy to contain something worth sharing. A life on fire is still a life. A frightened body is still trying to protect itself. A person who has fallen apart may still understand something about how pieces hold.

So I am beginning again—not from authority above the mess, but from inside it.
