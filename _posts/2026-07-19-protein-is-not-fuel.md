Protein is essential. The body uses amino acids to build and repair tissue and to make enzymes, transport proteins, immune proteins, and many signaling molecules. That does not make protein the body’s preferred everyday fuel.

Carbohydrate and fat are structured for energy storage and use. Amino acids contain nitrogen, which must be removed before much of their carbon structure can enter energy-producing pathways. The liver manages that nitrogen and converts it to urea for excretion.

The useful distinction is not that protein can *never* contribute energy. It can. The distinction is that using amino acids for energy requires additional processing and competes with their structural and functional roles.

## Why this matters

The internet often reduces nutrition to a single nutrient. Protein is currently marketed as though adding more is automatically better. But bodies require balance: sufficient energy, carbohydrate, fat, protein, vitamins, minerals, water, and a dietary pattern a person can actually maintain.

Learning this small piece of metabolism may not make you remember the urea cycle forever. It may, however, give you a stronger framework for questioning claims that treat protein as the answer to every nutrition problem.
