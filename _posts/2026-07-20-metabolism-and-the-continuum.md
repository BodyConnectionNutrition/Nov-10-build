Metabolism is usually taught as a collection of pathways to memorize. Nutrients enter. Enzymes act. Bonds break and form. Energy is transferred. Waste leaves. On a classroom diagram, the pathways can look mechanical and remote—arrows, abbreviations, cycles, and chemical structures detached from the person trying to learn them.

But metabolism is also a philosophical fact.

At this moment, matter that was recently outside you is becoming the activity you call yourself. Carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, minerals, and water move through reactions governed by the same physical laws that operate everywhere else. A carbohydrate made by a plant can be broken apart, its stored energy transferred and used to maintain an ion gradient across a neuron. That gradient can participate in a pattern that becomes a memory, a word, a fear, or the recognition of someone you love.

The world does not stop at the skin. It enters as air, food, light, sound, microbes, heat, and relationship. It leaves as breath, motion, heat, waste, speech, and changed conditions for other living things. The boundary is biologically useful, but it is not absolute. Life is maintained through exchange.

This is what I mean when I say that metabolism reveals us as part of a continuum. I do not mean that the universe intended to become conscious. I do not imagine a cosmic mind waiting to recognize itself through us. That would sneak purpose back into a process that does not require it. I mean something more literal: the matter and energy composing us have never belonged exclusively to us. For a while, physical processes take this particular organized form. The form experiences. Then the arrangement changes.

Several philosophical traditions approach parts of this picture. Spinoza rejected the separation of mind and nature, locating everything within one reality. Whitehead emphasized process over static substance. Naturalistic monists argued that life and mind do not require a second, supernatural kind of stuff. Merleau-Ponty described body and world as folds of the same flesh—not identical, but intertwined.

None of these thinkers gives me a doctrine I need to adopt whole. They provide language for a recognition that biochemistry had already made difficult to ignore: we are not visitors to nature. We are one of the things nature is doing.

This can sound grandiose, especially when phrased as “the universe experiencing itself.” But it is also profoundly decentering. Human consciousness is not the destination toward which matter has been traveling. We are not the reason stars formed or plants captured energy. Conscious experience is one astonishing outcome among innumerable outcomes. It is rare as far as we know, fragile in every individual body, and not required by the universe.

That contingency is part of what makes it precious.

Traditional spirituality often locates the sacred outside physical reality. Matter is temporary or fallen; the soul is the part that matters. I find the opposite more compelling. The complexity of metabolism—the capacity of matter, under physical law, to organize into life and eventually experience—is the only “God” I need. Not a being who planned the reactions, but the reactions themselves: lawful, creative without intention, and temporarily capable of wonder.

This view does not make suffering unreal. A continuum can still contain agony. The fact that a frightened nervous system is nature responding to nature does not make fear pleasant. Nor does interconnectedness erase the meaningful boundaries between people. My pain is not yours simply because both arise within one physical reality. Bodies organize experience locally. They protect their integrity. They can harm one another.

What the continuum changes is the story of alienation. If I am a soul trapped in a body, the body can become an obstacle, prison, or enemy. If I am an ongoing metabolic relationship with the world, the body is neither a vessel nor a possession. It is the temporary organization that makes this experience possible.

The same shift changes how death appears. It does not promise that personal consciousness continues. On the contrary, this particular experience depends on a particular living organization and will end when that organization can no longer be maintained. The matter does not vanish, but the person is not merely the matter. The person is the pattern, the history, the relationships, and the activity.

This offers no conventional immortality. It offers continuity without personal permanence.

For some people, that will feel insufficient. For me, it makes the present more urgent and more beautiful. The universe does not owe itself an audience. Yet here, briefly, matter can taste a peach, understand a sentence, feel grief, study its own chemistry, and love a child. Nothing had to arrange itself this way. Nothing guarantees it will continue.

Metabolism is not merely how the body turns food into energy. It is the daily evidence that the border between self and world is porous, active, and provisional. We eat the world. We breathe it. We become it, and it becomes something else through us.
